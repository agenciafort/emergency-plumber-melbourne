Gas vs Electric vs Heat Pump Hot Water: Which Is Best in Melbourne?

Replacing a hot water system in Melbourne? Gas, electric storage and heat pump each have different upfront costs, running costs and rebates. Here's an honest comparison to help you choose the right system for your home.

4 Apr 2026 9 min read Hot water · Melbourne

Answer first: For most Melbourne homes replacing a hot water system in 2026, a heat pump offers the lowest long-term running costs and qualifies for VIC rebates — despite higher upfront price. Gas continuous flow suits households with existing gas connections wanting instant hot water. Standard electric storage is cheapest to install but most expensive to run. Your best choice depends on household size, gas availability and budget horizon.

Hot water accounts for around 25% of household energy use in Victoria — often the second-largest energy cost after heating. Melbourne's temperate climate, established gas network in inner and middle suburbs, and generous state rebates for efficient systems make the choice more nuanced than simply picking the cheapest unit on the shelf.

This guide compares the three main options Melbourne plumbers install every day: gas (storage and continuous flow), electric storage, and heat pump systems — with honest running cost estimates, rebate information, and a decision table matched to household type.

How each hot water system works

Electric storage hot water

The most common system in older Melbourne homes. A large insulated tank (125–400 litres) heats water with one or two electric elements, maintaining temperature 24/7 — even when you're asleep or at work.

  • How it works: Thermostat triggers elements to heat water to ~60–70°C. Hot water rises, drawn from top of tank when you turn a tap.
  • Pros: Lowest upfront cost, simple installation, works anywhere with electricity.
  • Cons: Highest running costs — constantly reheating a full tank. Large footprint. Typical lifespan 10–12 years.

Gas hot water (storage and continuous flow)

Two gas options dominate Melbourne installations:

  • Gas storage: Similar to electric storage but heated by a gas burner under the tank. Faster recovery than electric. Requires a gas connection and flue venting.
  • Gas continuous flow (instantaneous): Heats water on demand as it flows through a heat exchanger — no storage tank. Compact wall-mounted unit. Popular in Melbourne townhouses and newer builds. Requires good gas pressure and flow rate for multiple simultaneous outlets.

Gas systems require a licensed gas fitter for installation. If you smell gas at any hot water unit, see our gas leak warning signs guide.

Heat pump hot water

Heat pumps are the fastest-growing category in Melbourne — and for good reason. They work like a reverse refrigerator: extract heat from ambient air and transfer it to water, using electricity only to run a compressor — not to directly heat elements.

  • How it works: A fan draws air across an evaporator, a compressor boosts refrigerant temperature, and a heat exchanger transfers energy to water in the tank. Works efficiently even in Melbourne's cooler months (down to around 0–5°C ambient).
  • Pros: 60–75% lower running costs than electric storage. Eligible for VIC and federal rebates. Lower emissions. Often same footprint as storage tank.
  • Cons: Higher upfront cost ($3,500–$5,500 installed before rebates). Requires adequate ventilation (often installed outdoors). Some models produce mild fan noise.

Running costs in Victoria

Annual hot water energy costs depend on household size, usage patterns, energy tariffs and system efficiency. These are indicative Melbourne/Victoria estimates for a typical 4-person household (150–200 litres hot water per day) at 2026 energy prices:

Tip: Running costs on time-of-use tariffs improve significantly if you heat water during off-peak periods (typically overnight on electric and heat pump systems). A licensed plumber can advise on tariff-optimised timer settings.

Rebates and incentives in Victoria

Melbourne homeowners replacing hot water systems can access several rebate programs that reduce upfront cost — particularly for efficient systems:

  • Solar Victoria — Hot Water Rebate

    Eligible Victorian households replacing an existing hot water system may receive rebates for heat pump and solar hot water systems. Check current amounts and eligibility at solar.vic.gov.au — income and property criteria apply.

  • Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU)

    Accredited providers offer point-of-sale discounts on eligible energy-efficient hot water systems under the VEU program. Your plumber or retailer may apply this directly — ask at quote stage.

  • Federal STCs (Small-scale Technology Certificates)

    Heat pump and solar hot water systems generate STCs based on expected energy displacement. Installers typically deduct STC value from the quoted price — worth $500–$1,000+ depending on system and zone.

Rebate programs change periodically. Always confirm current eligibility before purchasing — a reputable Melbourne plumber will factor available rebates into your quote.

Upfront installation costs in Melbourne (2026)

Installed prices vary by brand, capacity, location and complexity of changeover. Typical Melbourne ranges:

  • Electric storage (125–315L): $1,200 – $2,000
  • Gas storage: $1,400 – $2,500
  • Gas continuous flow: $1,800 – $3,500
  • Heat pump: $3,500 – $5,500 (before rebates)
  • Solar hot water (with boost): $4,000 – $7,000 (before rebates)

FAQ: Hot water system types in Melbourne

What is the cheapest hot water system to run in Melbourne?

Heat pump hot water systems are typically the cheapest to run, using 60–75% less electricity than conventional electric storage. Gas continuous flow is the next most economical for households with existing gas connections. Standard electric storage tanks are the most expensive to operate.

Are heat pump hot water systems worth it in Melbourne?

Yes, for most Melbourne households. Heat pumps work efficiently in Victoria's temperate climate, qualify for state and federal rebates, and reduce annual hot water energy costs by $300–$600 compared to electric storage. Higher upfront cost is usually recovered within 3–5 years.

Should I choose gas or electric hot water in Melbourne?

Choose gas if you have an existing natural gas connection and want lower running costs without heat pump upfront expense. Choose electric storage for lowest install cost. For new installations or replacements, heat pump electric is increasingly the best long-term choice due to rebates and running cost savings.

What hot water rebates are available in Victoria?

Victorian households may access Solar Victoria hot water rebates for eligible heat pump and solar systems, plus Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU) incentives and federal STCs. Check solar.vic.gov.au and energy.vic.gov.au for current eligibility and amounts.

How long does hot water system replacement take in Melbourne?

Like-for-like replacement (same type and location) typically takes 3–5 hours. Switching system types — especially gas to heat pump — may take a full day including electrical work. Same-day replacement is often available when you call a 24/7 Melbourne plumber.

Can I switch from gas to heat pump hot water?

Yes. Many Melbourne homeowners are switching from gas storage to heat pump during replacement, particularly as gas prices rise and rebates improve. You'll need adequate outdoor space with ventilation, a dedicated electrical circuit, and a licensed plumber and electrician. Your gas line can be capped by a licensed gas fitter.
