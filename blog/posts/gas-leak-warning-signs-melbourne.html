Gas Safety Guide

Do You Have a Gas Leak? Warning Signs to Act on Now

Natural gas leaks in Melbourne homes are rare but dangerous — and they never fix themselves. If you smell rotten eggs, hear hissing near a gas appliance, or feel unwell without explanation, this guide tells you exactly what to do in the first minutes.

21 Apr 2026 9 min read Gas leak · Melbourne

Answer first: If you smell rotten eggs, hear hissing near gas pipes or appliances, or notice dead vegetation around your gas meter — treat it as a gas leak. Do not use switches, flames or electrical appliances. Turn off the gas at the meter if safe, evacuate, and call 000 if there is immediate danger. From outside, call your gas distributor (1800 427 532 for metro Melbourne) and a licensed 24/7 gas fitter. Never sleep on a suspected gas leak.

Gas powers hot water, heating, cooktops and ovens in millions of Australian homes — including most of Melbourne's established suburbs. When installed and maintained correctly, natural gas is safe and efficient. But leaks from corroded pipework, loose connections, faulty appliances or damaged meters can release gas into your home — and because natural gas is lighter than air, it accumulates in ceiling spaces, cupboards and wall cavities where you may not immediately notice it.

This guide covers how to detect a gas leak using smell, sound and symptoms; the exact emergency steps to take; critical mistakes that can cause ignition; and who to call in Melbourne for a permanent repair.

How to detect a gas leak

Natural gas itself has no smell. Australian gas distributors add mercaptan — a harmless chemical with a distinctive rotten egg or sulphur odour — so leaks are detectable by humans. Detection relies on three senses plus visual clues around your property:

  • Smell — rotten eggs or sulphur

    The most common warning sign. Strongest near the gas meter, hot water unit, cooktop, heater or along exposed pipework. Smell that intensifies when an appliance is running is a red flag.

  • Sound — hissing or whistling

    A continuous hiss near the meter, regulator, appliance connection or underground pipe run suggests pressurised gas escaping through a crack or loose joint.

  • Symptoms — physical effects on people and pets

    Headache, dizziness, nausea, fatigue, breathing difficulty or flu-like symptoms without fever — especially if multiple household members feel unwell simultaneously indoors.

  • Visual — dead or dying vegetation

    Grass, plants or garden beds dying in a line above an underground gas service pipe — gas displaces oxygen in soil, killing roots.

  • Appliance clues — yellow or orange flames

    Gas burners should burn blue. Yellow, orange or flickering flames on a cooktop or heater can indicate incomplete combustion or a supply issue — call a licensed gas fitter.

  • Bill spike — unexplained gas usage increase

    A sudden rise in your gas bill without changed usage patterns may indicate a slow leak. Compare with the same period last year.

The smell, the sounds, the symptoms — in detail

The smell: rotten eggs near gas appliances

The mercaptan odour is deliberately strong — you should notice it before gas reaches dangerous concentrations in well-ventilated areas. Common Melbourne leak locations:

  • Gas meter and regulator — front boundary or side of house; check after gardening or construction near the meter box
  • Hot water unit — gas storage and continuous flow units; smell strongest when the burner ignites. See hot water system types if replacing a faulty gas unit
  • Cooktop and oven connections — behind or beneath the appliance where the flexible hose connects
  • Gas heater — ducted or room heaters, especially older units in Melbourne's period homes
  • Underground service line — smell may be faint indoors but stronger outdoors near the meter or along the pipe route

The sounds: hissing, whistling or roaring

Pressurised natural gas escaping through even a small opening creates audible noise. Listen near:

  • The gas meter shut-off valve and regulator
  • Flexible appliance hoses (these have a limited lifespan — typically 5–10 years)
  • Wall cavities where gas pipework runs (East Melbourne and inner-city terrace homes often have exposed or aged pipework)
  • Underground leaks — sometimes a faint roar at ground level near the service line

The symptoms: when gas affects your health

Natural gas displaces oxygen. In enclosed Melbourne rooms — especially bedrooms with closed windows during winter — low-level leaks can cause symptoms that mimic illness:

Symptom Common cause Action
Headache / dizziness Oxygen displacement from gas accumulation Evacuate immediately
Nausea / vomiting Prolonged low-level exposure indoors Fresh air, call 000 if severe
Difficulty breathing Higher gas concentration in enclosed space Call 000, do not re-enter
Fatigue / confusion Extended exposure, often overnight Evacuate, ventilate, call gas fitter
Pets unwell or lethargic Pets affected before humans in some cases Remove pets, investigate gas source

Carbon monoxide note: Incomplete combustion of gas appliances produces carbon monoxide (CO) — odourless and deadly. Symptoms overlap with gas leak symptoms. If you suspect CO from a faulty heater, evacuate immediately and call 000. Ensure all gas appliances are serviced annually by a licensed gas fitter.

What to do immediately if you suspect a gas leak

Follow these steps in order. Speed matters, but safety comes first — a spark in a gas-filled room can cause explosion or fire.

  1. Do not create ignition sources. No matches, lighters, smoking, light switches, power points, doorbells, mobile phones used inside the gas-affected area, or starting cars in attached garages.
  2. Turn off the gas at the meter — if safe. Your gas meter is usually at the front boundary or side of the house. The shut-off valve is a lever on the inlet pipe — turn it 90° so it sits perpendicular (crosswise) to the pipe. If the smell is overwhelming at the meter, skip this step and evacuate.
  3. Ventilate on your way out. Open external doors and windows as you leave — do not re-enter to ventilate further.
  4. Evacuate everyone. All people and pets leave the property and move upwind to fresh air.
  5. Call from a safe distance. Use a mobile phone or neighbour's phone well away from the building. Call 000 if there is fire, explosion risk, or anyone is seriously unwell. Call the gas distributor emergency line: 1800 427 532 (1800 GAS LEAK — Australian Gas Networks, metro Melbourne). Then call a licensed 24/7 gas fitter on [TELEFONE] for appliance and pipework repair.
  6. Do not re-enter until cleared. Wait for the gas fitter to locate the leak, repair pipework or appliances, pressure-test the system, and confirm it is safe.

What NOT to do during a gas leak

These mistakes cause injuries and property damage every year in Victoria. Avoid them completely:

  • Do not search for the leak with a naked flame

    Never use matches or lighters to "find" where gas is escaping — this is extremely dangerous and outdated advice.

  • Do not operate electrical switches indoors

    Light switches, power points, exhaust fans and garage door openers can create a spark sufficient to ignite gas.

  • Do not attempt DIY gas repairs

    Gas fitting is strictly regulated in Victoria under the Gas Safety Act. Only licensed gas fitters (Type A or Type B endorsed) can legally work on gas installations. Unlicensed work voids insurance.

  • Do not re-enter to collect belongings

    Gas concentration can change rapidly. Wait for professional clearance.

  • Do not ignore a faint smell

    Small leaks become large leaks. A faint rotten egg smell today may indicate a connection that fails completely tomorrow.

  • Do not wait until morning

    Gas leaks are always a true emergency. Natural gas accumulates overnight in enclosed Melbourne homes — see our guide on what counts as a plumbing emergency.

Common causes of gas leaks in Melbourne homes

  • Perished flexible hoses — cooktop and bayonet hoses have a limited lifespan; check the date stamp
  • Loose appliance connections — vibration from nearby traffic (common in inner Melbourne) can loosen fittings over time
  • Corroded pipework — older galvanised or steel pipes in pre-1970 homes in suburbs like North Melbourne and Footscray
  • Faulty hot water units — cracked heat exchangers or failed gas valves on ageing storage units
  • Damage during renovations — accidental nail or drill penetration of concealed gas lines
  • Faulty regulators or meters — distributor responsibility; report via 1800 427 532

FAQ: Gas leak warning signs in Melbourne

What does a gas leak smell like?

Natural gas is odourless, but Australian suppliers add mercaptan which smells like rotten eggs or sulphur. If you notice this smell near a gas appliance, meter or pipe — especially if it intensifies when appliances are running — treat it as a potential gas leak.

What should I do if I smell gas in my Melbourne home?

Do not use switches, flames or electrical appliances. Turn off the gas at the meter if safe, open doors on your way out, evacuate everyone, and call 000 if there is immediate danger. From outside, call 1800 427 532 (gas distributor) and a licensed 24/7 gas fitter. Do not re-enter until cleared.

Is a gas leak a plumbing emergency in Melbourne?

Yes — always. Gas leaks never fix themselves and can reach explosive concentrations in enclosed spaces. Licensed gas fitters respond 24/7. Never wait until morning.

Can I fix a gas leak myself?

No. Gas fitting is legally restricted to licensed professionals in Victoria. DIY repairs are illegal, void home insurance, and create serious safety risks. Only a licensed gas fitter can repair appliances and pipework and issue compliance certificates.

How do I turn off the gas at the meter in Melbourne?

Locate your gas meter (usually front boundary). Find the shut-off valve on the inlet pipe — a lever handle. Turn it 90 degrees so the lever sits perpendicular (crosswise) to the pipe. If you cannot reach the meter safely due to strong gas smell, evacuate without turning it off and call 000 or 1800 427 532 from outside.
