How to Clear a Blocked Drain in Melbourne (and When to Call a Pro)

Slow sink, gurgling shower or smelly floor drain? Many blocked drains in Melbourne homes can be cleared with safe DIY steps — but some need professional jet blasting, CCTV inspection or root removal. Here's how to tell the difference.

Blocked drains are among the most common plumbing calls across Melbourne — from hair-clogged bathroom basins in St Kilda apartments to tree-rooted sewer lines in Glen Waverley garden suburbs. The right response depends on whether you're dealing with a local trap blockage (often DIY-friendly) or a main line problem (always professional).

This guide covers the warning signs, safe DIY methods, why chemical cleaners do more harm than good, the real causes behind Melbourne blockages, when to call a pro, and what drain clearing actually costs.

Signs of a blocked drain

Catching a blockage early — before it becomes a sewer backup — saves money and mess. Watch for these warning signs:

  • Slow drainage

    Water pools in the sink, shower or basin and takes noticeably longer to empty. The most common early sign — often weeks before a full blockage.

  • Gurgling sounds

    Air trapped in the pipe escapes as water drains, creating gurgling from the plughole, toilet or floor waste. Indicates partial blockage or venting issue.

  • Bad odours

    Rotten egg or sewage smell from drains — organic matter decomposing in a blocked trap or sewer line.

  • Water backing up

    Water rising in the shower when you flush the toilet, or the kitchen sink filling when the dishwasher runs. Strong sign of main line blockage.

  • Toilet bubbles when basin runs

    Running the bathroom tap causes the toilet bowl water to bubble or rise — shared drain line is partially blocked.

Single fixture slow = likely local blockage. Multiple fixtures affected = call a plumber. Don't spend an hour plunging a kitchen sink when the real problem is tree roots 15 metres down the sewer line.

Plunger method — the safest DIY fix

A plunger clears many simple blockages within 10 minutes if used correctly. The technique differs slightly for sinks, showers and toilets.

Kitchen or bathroom sink

  1. Remove the plug and clear visible debris (hair, food scraps) from the grate.
  2. If your sink has an overflow hole (small hole near the top), seal it with a wet cloth — otherwise plunging pushes air out the overflow instead of down the drain.
  3. Fill the basin with enough water to cover the plunger cup.
  4. Place a flat cup plunger over the drain and push down firmly, then pull up sharply. Repeat 10–15 times without breaking the seal.
  5. Remove the plunger and check if water drains freely. Repeat once if needed.

Shower drain

Remove the drain cover if possible. Use a cup plunger directly over the opening. Hair blockages near the surface often clear with plunging combined with manual removal using needle-nose pliers or a zip-it drain tool.

Toilet (use a flange plunger)

Toilets need a flange plunger — not a flat sink plunger. See our dedicated guide: why is my toilet overflowing.

Why avoid chemical drain cleaners

Supermarket drain cleaners are marketed as a quick fix. In practice, they're one of the worst things you can pour down a Melbourne drain — and plumbers nationwide consistently advise against them.

  • They damage pipes

    Caustic and acid-based cleaners generate heat and corrosive reactions. They soften PVC joints, eat chrome fittings, and accelerate corrosion in old galvanised and clay pipes common in Melbourne's inner suburbs.

  • They rarely fix the real problem

    Chemicals might dissolve a small grease plug near the surface but cannot remove tree roots, collapsed pipe sections, or solid objects. You waste money and time while the underlying blockage worsens.

  • They endanger plumbers

    When DIY chemicals fail and you call a professional, residual toxic chemicals in the line create splash and fume hazards during jet blasting or snaking. Plumbers may charge extra for hazardous waste disposal.

  • They harm the environment

    Chemical drain products enter Melbourne's wastewater system and can disrupt treatment processes. Professional mechanical clearing (jet blasting, electric eel) is chemical-free.

Professional high-pressure water jetting clears blockages mechanically — no chemicals, no pipe damage, and it scours the pipe wall to reduce recurrence. That's the standard approach used by licensed Melbourne drain plumbers.

Common causes of blocked drains in Melbourne

Understanding what's blocking your drain helps you prevent the next one — and tells your plumber what equipment to bring.

  • Tree roots

    Melbourne's established suburbs — Hawthorn, Camberwell, Glen Waverley, Williamstown — have mature trees whose roots seek moisture in clay sewer pipes through tiny joints. The number-one cause of main line blockages. Read tree roots in your pipes.

  • Fat, oil and grease (FOG)

    Poured down kitchen sinks, fat solidifies in pipes as it cools — especially in Melbourne's winter months. Builds into stubborn "fatbergs" that need jet blasting.

  • "Flushable" wipes and hygiene products

    Wipes don't break down like toilet paper. They snag in pipes and trap other debris. Never flush wipes, sanitary products, cotton buds or dental floss.

  • Hair and soap scum

    Bathroom basin and shower blockages — hair binds with soap residue near the trap. Usually a local, DIY-fixable blockage.

  • Collapsed or cracked pipes

    Melbourne's ageing clay and earthenware sewer lines (common pre-1980) crack, shift and collapse over decades — especially after ground movement or heavy vehicle traffic over buried lines.

When to call a plumber

Stop DIY and call a licensed Melbourne drain plumber if any of these apply:

  • Multiple fixtures blocked — toilet, shower and basin all slow or backing up
  • Sewage smell or visible sewage — health hazard, 24/7 emergency
  • DIY failed after two genuine attempts — the blockage is deeper than the trap
  • Recurring blockages — same drain blocks every few months (likely roots or pipe damage)
  • Water backing up into the home — floor drains overflowing, water spreading to other rooms
  • Stormwater flooding — outdoor drains blocked during Melbourne rain events

Professional drain clearing in Melbourne uses electric eels (drain snakes) for solid blockages, high-pressure jetters for grease and roots, and CCTV cameras to inspect pipe condition after clearing. FAQ: Blocked drains in Melbourne

How do I know if my drain is blocked?

Signs include slow-draining sinks or showers, gurgling sounds, unpleasant odours, water pooling around floor drains, and toilets that bubble when you run the basin. Multiple fixtures affected at once usually means a main sewer line blockage requiring professional clearing.

Can I clear a blocked drain myself?

Yes, for simple single-fixture blockages near the trap. Use a plunger with a proper seal, remove visible debris, and try hot water with dish soap for kitchen grease. Do not use chemical drain cleaners — they damage pipes and rarely fix the root cause.

Why should I avoid chemical drain cleaners?

Chemical cleaners generate heat and corrosive reactions that damage PVC pipes, old clay sewers and chrome fittings. They often fail on solid blockages like tree roots, and create toxic hazards for plumbers who later work on the line. Professional jet blasting is safer and more effective.

What causes blocked drains in Melbourne?

The most common causes are tree root intrusion in clay sewer pipes, fat and grease buildup in kitchen lines, "flushable" wipes and hygiene products, hair and soap scum in bathroom drains, and collapsed or cracked pipes in older Melbourne homes.

How much does drain clearing cost in Melbourne?

Simple drain clearing costs $200–$450 during business hours. High-pressure jet blasting for grease or roots ranges from $300–$650. Main sewer line clearing with multiple fixtures affected costs $350–$800+. After-hours rates add 50–100%.

How often should drains be professionally cleaned?

Most Melbourne homes don't need scheduled drain cleaning unless you have recurring blockages, mature trees near sewer lines, or a pre-1980 clay pipe system. If the same drain blocks more than twice a year, CCTV inspection is recommended to identify root intrusion or pipe damage before it becomes an emergency.
