Tree Roots in Your Pipes: Signs, Risks & Solutions

Tree root blockages are the number-one cause of recurring sewer problems in Melbourne's established suburbs. Here's how roots get in, what to watch for, how plumbers diagnose and remove them — and how to stop them coming back.

11 Apr 2026 8 min read Tree roots · Blocked drains

Answer first: Tree roots enter Melbourne sewer pipes through cracks and joints in ageing clay lines, then grow until they block the pipe completely. Warning signs include recurring blockages, gurgling drains and lush patches above the sewer run. Removal requires professional jet cutting or mechanical snaking — DIY and chemical cleaners won't work. Pipe relining prevents recurrence without digging.

If you live in a Melbourne suburb with mature trees — Glen Waverley, Hawthorn, Camberwell, Williamstown, Yarraville or Box Hill — and your drains keep blocking every few months despite plunging and clearing, tree roots are the likely culprit. Melbourne's pre-1980 housing stock is full of original clay and earthenware sewer pipes that roots love.

Unlike a one-off grease blockage, root intrusion is a progressive problem that worsens every season until the pipe is fully obstructed or collapses. Understanding how roots get in, recognising early signs, and choosing the right professional solution saves thousands in emergency call-outs and excavation.

How tree roots get into sewer pipes

Tree roots don't randomly punch through solid pipes. They exploit weaknesses that develop over decades in Melbourne's underground sewer infrastructure.

The entry process:

  1. Moisture detection: Fine root hairs detect moisture vapour escaping through tiny cracks, loose joints or porous sections in clay pipes — especially during Melbourne's dry summer months when trees are actively seeking water.
  2. Joint penetration: Most Melbourne clay sewer lines were laid in 1–1.2 metre sections joined with cement mortar or rubber rings. Over 40–80 years, joints shift, mortar crumbles and rubber perishes — creating gaps as small as 1–2 mm.
  3. Root growth inside the pipe: Once inside, roots expand in the nutrient-rich sewer environment. They spread like a net across the pipe diameter, trapping toilet paper, wipes and debris until flow stops entirely.
  4. Pipe damage accelerates: Growing roots exert pressure on pipe walls, widening cracks and eventually causing sections to collapse or displace — particularly under driveways and near large trees like figs, elms and oak species common in Melbourne gardens.

High-risk Melbourne scenarios: Large trees within 3–5 metres of the sewer line · Pre-1970 clay pipes · Properties in established garden suburbs · Trees planted directly above known sewer runs · Previous blockages cleared but roots not fully removed.

Warning signs of tree roots in your drains

Root blockages develop slowly — often over months or years. These signs appear before a full sewer backup:

  • Recurring blockages

    The same drain blocks every 3–6 months despite clearing. The classic root signature — each clearance removes surface debris but leaves the root mass intact to regrow.

  • Gurgling drains and toilets

    Air trapped by partial root obstruction escapes as gurgling when water drains. Toilets may bubble when you run the basin or shower.

  • Unusually lush grass or garden patches

    A greener, faster-growing section of lawn or garden directly above the sewer line — roots inside the pipe leak moisture and nutrients into surrounding soil.

  • Sewage odours from floor wastes

    Partial blockages trap organic matter that decomposes, releasing smells from laundry floor drains or outdoor grates.

  • Multiple fixtures affected

    Toilet, shower and basin all slow simultaneously — the blockage is in the shared main sewer line, not a local trap.

  • Blockages worsen after rain

    Roots absorb more water and expand after Melbourne rain events, temporarily narrowing the pipe further.

CCTV drain camera diagnosis

You can't see tree roots from above ground — and guessing leads to repeat call-outs. Professional CCTV drain inspection is the definitive diagnostic tool for Melbourne root problems.

How CCTV drain inspection works:

  • A waterproof HD camera on a flexible rod is fed into the sewer line from an access point (inspection opening, toilet or external grate).
  • The camera records live footage of the pipe interior — showing root masses, cracked joints, displaced sections and pipe material.
  • The plumber locates the blockage precisely using a sonde (electronic locator) that transmits the camera's position to the surface — so you know exactly where on your property the problem sits.
  • Footage is saved and can be shared with insurers, builders or for relining quotes.

CCTV inspection typically costs $250–$450 in Melbourne and is worth every dollar if you've had more than one blockage in the same line. It answers the critical question: Are roots the problem, and is the pipe structurally sound enough to reline — or does it need replacement?

Removal and pipe relining solutions

Clearing roots is only half the job — without addressing the entry point, they'll grow back within 6–18 months. Melbourne plumbers use a staged approach:

Step 1: High-pressure water jetting

A specialised jetter sends high-pressure water (up to 5,000 PSI) through a rotating nozzle that cuts through root masses inside the pipe. Unlike mechanical snakes that punch a hole through roots, jetting scours the pipe wall clean. Cost: typically $350–$650 depending on blockage severity and access.

Step 2: Mechanical drain snake (electric eel)

For heavily compacted root balls or where jetting access is limited, a powered drain snake with cutting attachments removes root material. Often used in combination with jetting for thorough clearance.

Step 3: Pipe relining (trenchless repair)

If CCTV reveals cracked, displaced or root-damaged pipe sections, pipe relining creates a seamless new pipe inside the old one — without digging up your garden, driveway or lawn.

  • An epoxy-impregnated liner is inserted into the damaged section and inflated against the pipe wall.
  • The epoxy cures to form a hard, root-resistant inner pipe with no joints for roots to enter.
  • Relining typically costs $1,500–$4,000+ per section depending on length and diameter — far less than full excavation and replacement.
  • Warranty periods of 10–25 years are common on quality relining work.

Step 4: Full pipe replacement (last resort)

If the pipe has collapsed, severely displaced or is beyond relining repair, excavation and replacement may be necessary. This is the most expensive option ($3,000–$10,000+ depending on depth, access and surface restoration) and is why early CCTV diagnosis and relining are strongly preferred in Melbourne's established suburbs.

Prevention — stopping roots before they block

Once roots have entered a pipe, they'll keep coming back unless the entry point is sealed. Prevention strategies for Melbourne homeowners:

  • Know your sewer line location

    Request a sewer diagram from your water retailer (Yarra Valley Water, South East Water, etc.) or check your building plans. Avoid planting large trees within 3 metres of the line.

  • Choose sewer-safe trees

    Avoid figs, elms, willows and poplars near sewer runs — aggressive root systems. Prefer smaller native species with less invasive roots for garden beds near pipes.

  • CCTV inspect before buying

    Pre-purchase drain inspections are essential for Melbourne homes built before 1980. Root damage discovered after settlement becomes your expensive problem.

  • Recurring blockages = act early

    If the same drain blocks twice in 12 months, don't keep paying for temporary clears. CCTV + relining costs less than three emergency call-outs plus eventual excavation.

  • Never flush wipes or hygiene products

    Roots trap debris — reducing what enters the pipe slows blockage formation even when roots are present.

FAQ: Tree roots in Melbourne sewer pipes

How do tree roots get into sewer pipes in Melbourne?

Tree roots enter through tiny cracks, joints and fractures in ageing clay and earthenware lines — common in pre-1980 Melbourne homes. Roots seek moisture and nutrients inside the pipe, growing thicker over time until they block the entire diameter.

What are the warning signs of tree roots in drains?

Warning signs include slow-draining fixtures, gurgling toilets, recurring blockages every few months, lush green patches above the sewer line, and sewage smells from floor wastes. Multiple fixtures backing up together strongly indicates a main line root blockage.

Can tree roots be removed from pipes without digging?

Yes. Licensed Melbourne plumbers use high-pressure water jetters and mechanical drain snakes to cut roots from inside the pipe. CCTV inspection confirms clearance. For damaged pipes, trenchless pipe relining creates a new lining inside the existing pipe — no excavation required.

Will roots grow back after clearing?

Yes, unless the entry point is sealed. Jet cutting alone provides temporary relief — roots typically regrow within 6–18 months. Pipe relining is the permanent solution, creating a seamless inner pipe with no joints for roots to penetrate.

How much does tree root removal cost in Melbourne?

High-pressure jet root cutting costs $350–$650 during business hours. CCTV inspection adds $250–$450. Pipe relining ranges from $1,500–$4,000+ depending on section length. After-hours emergency rates add 50–100%. See our cost guide for full pricing.

Which Melbourne suburbs are most affected by tree root blockages?

Established garden suburbs with pre-1980 clay sewer pipes and mature trees — including Glen Waverley, Hawthorn, Camberwell, Box Hill, Williamstown, Yarraville and Kew — have the highest incidence. We service all Melbourne suburbs — see our areas we cover.
