Hot Water Guide

Signs Your Hot Water System Is About to Fail

A cold shower on a Melbourne winter morning is bad enough — but some hot water warning signs mean more than inconvenience. Here's how to spot a failing unit before it floods your laundry or leaves you without hot water entirely.

7 Apr 2026 8 min read Hot water · Melbourne

Answer first: Your hot water system is likely failing if you have no hot water, rusty or discoloured water from hot taps, banging or rumbling from the unit, water pooling at the tank base, or the system is over 10–12 years old. A leak at the base almost always means replace — not repair. Catch warning signs early to avoid emergency floods and after-hours premiums.

Hot water systems don't fail without warning — they give you signs for weeks or months before the tank ruptures or the burner dies completely. In Melbourne, where winter mornings drop below 5°C and storage tanks sit in unheated laundries and outdoor cupboards, system stress is real.

Knowing what to watch for lets you book a same-day repair instead of a panicked 2am call when the tank finally splits. This guide covers every major warning sign, what each one means, and the repair-vs-replace decision that saves Melbourne homeowners thousands.

No hot water — or water that won't stay hot

The most obvious sign — and the one that triggers most emergency calls. But the details matter for diagnosis:

  • Completely cold: No hot water from any hot tap. On electric systems, likely a failed heating element or thermostat. On gas systems, could be a extinguished pilot light, failed thermocouple, or gas valve fault.
  • Lukewarm only: Water warm but never hot enough for a comfortable shower. Often a failing lower element (electric), partial thermostat failure, or undersized continuous-flow unit struggling with demand.
  • Hot then cold quickly: Starts hot but runs cold within minutes. Classic sign of sediment buildup at the bottom of a storage tank — the element heats sediment instead of water, reducing effective capacity.
  • Hot water runs out faster than before: A family of four used to get through morning showers fine — now the tank empties after two. Sediment displacement or a failing element reducing heating efficiency.

If you have a gas hot water system and smell gas near the unit, do not attempt to relight the pilot yourself — evacuate, ventilate and call a licensed gas fitter immediately. See our gas leak warning signs guide.

Rusty or discoloured hot water

Brown, orange or rusty water from hot taps only (cold taps clear) is one of the clearest indicators that your storage tank is corroding from the inside out.

What's happening: Steel storage tanks have a glass or enamel lining that degrades over time. Melbourne's water — particularly in older suburbs with ageing mains infrastructure — can accelerate corrosion once the lining fails. Rust particles mix with hot water and come out your taps.

What to do: Run a hot tap for 30 seconds into a white bucket. If water is consistently discoloured, the tank is corroding internally. A sacrificial anode rod (designed to attract corrosion instead of the tank wall) may be depleted — sometimes replaceable on units under 8 years. On older units, rusty water usually means replacement is imminent.

Discoloured water from both hot and cold taps suggests a problem with Melbourne's mains supply, not your hot water unit — contact your water retailer.

Strange noises from the hot water unit

Your hot water system should operate quietly. These sounds indicate problems:

  • Banging or rumbling

    Sediment and mineral scale have hardened at the bottom of the storage tank. When the element or burner heats this layer, steam bubbles form and collapse violently — causing rumbling. Common in Melbourne's hard water areas and units over 8 years without flushing.

  • Popping or crackling

    Similar cause — scale buildup on the heating element itself (electric) or heat exchanger (gas). The element may be failing under the scale layer.

  • Hissing or sizzling

    Water dripping onto a hot burner or element — indicates an internal leak. Investigate immediately; this often precedes a tank split.

  • Whistling or high-pitched sound

    Pressure relief valve (PRV) releasing excess pressure — may indicate thermostat failure causing overheating. A safety concern on gas systems.

Leaks at the base of the unit

This is the most urgent warning sign. Water pooling beneath a storage hot water tank — even a small amount — demands immediate attention.

Leak from a fitting or valve (top or side of unit): May be repairable — often a loose pressure relief valve, inlet/outlet connection, or drain valve. A licensed plumber can usually fix these same-day for $150–$350.

Leak from the tank base itself: The tank wall has corroded through. This is not repairable — the structural integrity of the tank is compromised and it can rupture fully at any time, flooding your laundry or cupboard. Replacement is required.

Critical: A leaking hot water tank can release 100–300+ litres rapidly when it fails completely. Turn off the water inlet to the unit and call a plumber immediately. Place towels and a bucket under the leak while you wait.

Age of the unit — Melbourne lifespan guide

Hot water systems have finite lifespans. If you don't know your unit's age, check the compliance plate on the side — it shows manufacture date and model number.

Melbourne's mix of hard and soft water across suburbs affects lifespan. Units in western suburbs with higher mineral content may scale faster without regular maintenance. If your unit is approaching end-of-life and showing any other warning sign, start planning replacement before an emergency failure.

FAQ: Failing hot water systems in Melbourne

What are the signs your hot water system is failing?

Warning signs include no hot water or inconsistent temperature, rusty or discoloured water from hot taps, banging or rumbling noises from the unit, water pooling at the base of the tank, and a unit age exceeding 10–12 years for storage systems. Any leak at the base usually means the tank has corroded through and needs replacement.

Should I repair or replace my hot water system?

Repair if the unit is under 8 years old and the fault is a replaceable part — thermostat, element, gas valve or pressure relief valve. Replace if the tank is leaking from the base, over 10–12 years old, producing rusty water consistently, or repair costs exceed 50% of a new unit.

Is no hot water a plumbing emergency in Melbourne?

No hot water alone is urgent but not usually a true emergency unless the unit is leaking, you smell gas, or you have vulnerable household members. Same-day service is recommended. See what counts as a plumbing emergency.

How long do hot water systems last in Melbourne?

Electric storage tanks last 10–12 years. Gas storage units last 8–12 years. Continuous flow gas units last 15–20 years. Heat pump systems last 10–15 years. Replace the sacrificial anode rod every 5 years to extend storage tank life.

Why is my hot water brown or rusty?

Rusty hot water (with clear cold water) means internal tank corrosion. The glass lining and anode rod have failed and rust is mixing with your hot water. On units over 10 years, replacement is usually the most economical option. On younger units, an anode replacement may extend life.

How much does hot water system replacement cost in Melbourne?

Installed replacement costs typically range from $1,200–$2,000 for electric storage, $1,400–$2,500 for gas storage, $1,800–$3,500 for gas continuous flow, and $3,500–$5,500 for heat pump systems (before VIC rebates). Emergency after-hours installation adds 50–100%. See our cost guide.
