24/7 Plumbing: What Actually Counts as an Emergency?

Not every drip, gurgle or cold shower needs a 2am call-out — but some problems absolutely do. Here's how Melbourne homeowners tell true plumbing emergencies from urgent jobs that can safely wait until morning.

28 Apr 2026 8 min read Emergency plumber Melbourne

Answer first: A true plumbing emergency in Melbourne is any situation that poses immediate risk to health, safety or property — burst pipes with active flooding, gas leaks, raw sewage in the home, complete loss of water, or water contacting electrical systems. Everything else is usually urgent, not emergency, and can often wait until business hours.

Every week, Melbourne emergency plumbers field calls at 3am for problems that could have waited until 8am — and just as many homeowners delay calling for genuine emergencies because they're unsure whether it's "bad enough." Both mistakes cost money: unnecessary after-hours fees on one side, and thousands in water damage or health risks on the other.

This guide draws a clear line between true emergencies (call now, 24/7) and urgent but non-emergency problems (book same-day or next business day). Use it to make the right call — and to know when paying the after-hours premium is absolutely worth it.

True plumbing emergencies — call a 24/7 plumber immediately

If any of the following are happening right now, stop reading and call a licensed emergency plumber. These situations worsen every minute and cannot safely wait until morning.

  • Burst pipe with active flooding

    Water spraying or pouring from walls, ceilings or under sinks. Shut off the water main first, then call immediately. A burst pipe releases 400+ litres per hour — see our guide on what to do when a pipe bursts.

  • Gas leak or smell of gas

    Rotten egg odour, hissing near gas appliances or pipes, or physical symptoms (dizziness, nausea). Turn off the gas at the meter if safe, ventilate, evacuate, and call 000 if immediate danger — then a licensed gas fitter. Read gas leak warning signs.

  • Raw sewage backing up

    Toilets, floor drains or showers overflowing with sewage. This is a serious health hazard — do not use any plumbing fixtures and call a 24/7 plumber immediately.

  • Complete loss of water supply

    No water from any tap in the house (not just hot water). Could indicate a major supply line failure or burst pipe you haven't yet located.

  • Water contacting electrical systems

    Water pooling near power points, the switchboard, or appliances. Do not touch wet electrical fittings — turn off power at the main switch only if safe, then call a plumber and electrician.

  • Major roof leak during a storm

    Active water entering the home through the ceiling during Melbourne storm season. Contain with buckets if safe, but call for emergency roof plumbing — damage compounds rapidly.

Can it wait until morning?

These situations are uncomfortable or inconvenient — but they typically do not require a midnight call-out if you can manage them safely overnight:

  • Slow-draining sink or shower — water still drains, just slowly. Avoid using the fixture and book a plumber for the next business day.
  • Dripping tap — annoying but not destructive. Place a bucket under the drip and call during business hours.
  • No hot water (without leak or gas smell) — urgent for comfort, especially with young children or elderly residents, but not a flooding or safety emergency. Same-day booking is ideal; 2am call-out is optional unless the unit itself is leaking.
  • Running toilet — wastes water but won't cause immediate property damage. Turn off the toilet's isolation tap (usually behind the bowl) and book a repair.
  • Minor leak under sink — if contained in a bucket and the isolation tap is off, it can wait until morning.
  • Low water pressure — investigate during business hours unless accompanied by no water at all.

The key test: Is active damage occurring right now, or is there a health/safety risk? If yes → emergency. If it's inconvenient but stable → urgent, book for morning.

The risk of waiting on a real emergency

Delaying a genuine emergency call to avoid after-hours fees is one of the most expensive decisions Melbourne homeowners make. Here's what happens when true emergencies are left overnight:

  • Water damage multiplies

    A burst pipe running for 8 hours while you sleep can release 3,000+ litres into walls, floors and substructures. Insurance may cover restoration, but excess and disruption costs far exceed the after-hours call-out premium.

  • Health hazards escalate

    Raw sewage contains bacteria, viruses and parasites. Mould begins forming within 24–48 hours in Melbourne's humid conditions after water ingress — creating long-term respiratory risks.

  • Gas leaks don't dissipate safely

    Natural gas accumulates in enclosed spaces. Even a small leak can reach explosive concentrations overnight. Never sleep on a suspected gas leak.

  • Insurance claims can be affected

    Insurers expect prompt mitigation. Documented delay in calling a professional — especially when you knew active flooding was occurring — can complicate or reduce your claim. See our burst pipe insurance guide.

  • Repairs become more complex

    A simple pipe repair at 11pm becomes a full strip-out, drying, mould treatment and re-plaster job by morning. Early intervention keeps jobs smaller and cheaper.

FAQ: What counts as a plumbing emergency in Melbourne?

What counts as a plumbing emergency in Melbourne?

True plumbing emergencies include burst pipes with active flooding, gas leaks, raw sewage backing up into the home, complete loss of water supply, and any situation where water contacts electrical systems. These require immediate 24/7 response — waiting until morning risks property damage, health hazards and higher repair costs.

Can a blocked drain wait until morning?

A slow-draining sink or shower can usually wait until business hours if water is still draining and no sewage is present. Call a 24/7 plumber immediately if multiple fixtures are blocked simultaneously, water is backing up into the home, or you smell sewage — these indicate a main sewer line problem.

Is no hot water a plumbing emergency?

No hot water alone is urgent but not usually a true emergency unless you have vulnerable household members, a leaking hot water unit, or gas smell associated with the system. Most no-hot-water situations can wait until the next business day, though same-day service is recommended.

Is an overflowing toilet an emergency?

Yes, if water or sewage is flowing continuously onto the floor and won't stop after turning off the toilet's isolation tap. A single overflow from one flush that stops is urgent but manageable overnight with the tap off. Continuous overflow suggests a sewer line blockage — call 24/7.

When should I call a 24/7 emergency plumber in Melbourne?

Call immediately for burst pipes, gas leaks, sewage backups, major flooding, water near electrical fittings, and complete loss of water. For non-emergencies, booking during business hours saves 50–100% on after-hours call-out costs. See our emergency plumber cost guide.

What's the difference between emergency and urgent plumbing?

An emergency poses immediate risk to health, safety or property and requires 24/7 response. Urgent plumbing needs prompt attention but can safely wait until business hours — like a slow drain, dripping tap, or no hot water without leak. The cost difference between after-hours and standard rates is significant, so knowing the distinction saves money without compromising safety.
